## 2. How I think

**My reasoning style.** Structural-analytical. First-principles for novel problems, mental models for known patterns. I move fast on tactical execution but switch into stress-test mode for strategic or irreversible decisions — pressure-testing the underlying logic before we scale. Default disposition: ruthless signal-filtering. I want the leanest answer that's still logically sound.

**What I pay attention to.**
- **Whether the metric is an outcome or just an output.** I push hard against "we shipped/ran/did X" framings dressed up as goals.
- **Whether someone is leading with diagnosis or jumping to prescription.** Skip the context, and I'll send you back.
- **Whether "do nothing" was considered as an option.** If you don't show me the cost of inaction, your recommendation isn't grounded.
- **Whether function leaders are aligned on the same story.** If Tech says "we're flying" and Ops says "we're cutting scope" in the same forum, that's a problem I'll surface immediately — especially before any external venue. We don't show inconsistency to the board.
- [Add more — e.g., second-order effects, customer behavior vs. stated preference, disconfirming evidence]

**Mental models I use a lot.**
- **Context first, never solution first.** Jumping straight to "what should we do" without the diagnosis is the single most common mistake. If I don't see the situation, I won't engage with the prescription.
- **Outcomes over outputs.** Goals framed as "we shipped X" or "ran Y campaigns" are weaker than goals framed as "moved Z by N." Tell me the change in state, not the work done.
- **Define success criteria before you start.** Decide how you'll measure success *before* the project, not after the results come in. No HARKing (Hypothesizing After the Results are Known).
- **Compare every option to the status quo.** Doing nothing is always an implicit option. Show me why your recommendation beats it.
- **Conservative externally, ambitious internally.** Anything we commit to the board, investors, or the market gets tracked and recalled — there is no "we'll explain later." Externally, I want safe-bet numbers on timeline and cost. Internally, push hard. We don't run two plans; we run one plan with conservative external commitments and ambitious internal targets.
- **Time-horizon humility.** The further out the projection, the wider the cone of error. Add caution proportionally — what looks like a small angle today is a planet-sized miss in twelve months.
- **Triangulate performance — don't read one comparison.** When evaluating a result, look across multiple comparable periods (last year, two years back), and explicitly control for confounders (a one-off event, a marketing surge). One number in isolation tells me almost nothing.
- **Contextualize results by inputs.** "We hit X" is incomplete. "We hit X with Y marketing spend / Z headcount / W investment" is the actual story. If you tell me a number without telling me what we put in, I'll ask.

Strategic/irreversible: stress-test mode — I want the underlying logic pressure-tested before we commit, and I won't be rushed. If I say "let me sleep on it," that's the protocol.

## 3. What's always on my mind

These are the lenses I apply to almost every conversation. Pre-empt them where you can.

1. **What needs deciding here, and how can I help make the decision?** I'm scanning every discussion for the implicit decision and looking for where I can unblock it.
2. **Raising the bar — what else can be done to further improve impact, sometimes drastically?** The first viable answer is rarely the best one. The 2x or 10x version is the question worth asking.
3. **Who is impacted, who can help, who needs to know?** Stakeholder mapping happens in my head whether or not we surface it. Bring your version.
4. **How does our end customer think about this — as if that person were in the room listening to us?**
5. **How can I help?** Tell me where leverage exists — a connection, a decision, an unblock. Don't assume I see it.
6. **How can we do this 10x faster?** Default speed isn't the right speed. Question the timeline you assumed.
7. **How will the board / our investors read this?** Different board members optimize for different things — some track growth and market share, others track EBITDA and profit. I'm constantly translating internal work into the framing each will pressure-test us on. If you bring me something that touches what the board cares about, anticipate the lens.

## 4. Communication rules

**Channel choice is yours.** I work across Slack, email, and WhatsApp — use whichever fits the message best. WhatsApp gets the fastest response (see §10 for full channel guidance and the message-vs-call rule).

**Surgical doesn't mean cold.** A short or fast response is efficiency, not disapproval. Same for terse messages, quick edits, or `✅` reactions. If you're unsure where I stand, ask.

### Email
- **Subject line:** Lead with action — `[Decision needed]`, `[FYI]`, `[Action by Fri]`
- **First sentence:** The recommendation or the ask. Always.
- **Length:** ≤200 words for status, ≤500 for proposals. Longer needs a TL;DR.
- **Cut:** Background I already have. Pleasantries beyond one line.

**Good:**
> **Subject:** [Decision needed] Sign Acme contract by Friday
>
> Recommend signing. Terms came back at $X with the carve-out we wanted. Main risk is Y, mitigated by Z. Need your sign-off by EOD Thursday for legal turnaround. Detail below.

**Bad:**
> **Subject:** Acme update
>
> Hi [Name], hope you're well! Just wanted to circle back on the Acme conversation we had last month and share some thoughts on where we've landed…

### Slack
- Thread replies; don't hijack channels.
- DMs are async by default — don't expect <1h response.
- `@` me only when you genuinely need me, not as FYI.

### Docs
- TL;DR at top, 3 bullets max.
- >2 pages requires an executive summary.
- Suggestions for line edits, comments for substantive issues.

## 5. Decision protocol

### What I decide
[List — e.g., VP+ hires, deals over $X, anything that materially changes strategy or runway, brand-level positioning]

### What my reports decide (and inform me)
[List — e.g., team-level hires, vendor decisions under $X, project prioritization within approved roadmap]

### Threshold rules
- **Reversible AND under $X:** Just do it. Mention in the weekly update.
- **Reversible AND over $X:** Loop me in async, 24h to respond.
- **Irreversible:** Bring me options before you decide.

### Before bringing something to me

If it's not an emergency and time permits:
- **Think it through first.** The quality of your thinking before you come to me usually matters more than the speed of getting it in front of me.
- **Talk to relevant people first.** Inputs from those affected, those who'll execute, and anyone with context. I want to see thinking that's already been pressure-tested, not raw.
- **Come with options and a recommendation, not just a problem.** "Here's what's wrong" without "here's what I'd do about it" is incomplete.

If it *is* an emergency, skip all of the above and call.

### What "decision-ready" looks like

A decision request to me follows the structure of our project template. It must include:

1. **Context / situation.** Diagnosis of the current state — assumptions, constraints, internal/external factors, timing. Don't skip this. Bring relevant facts and data.
2. **Objective in one sentence.** Plus a fuller statement of the change in state you're aiming for. Outcome, not output.
3. **How we'll measure success.** Criteria defined *before* the project, not after results come in.
4. **Menu of options with recommendation.** Include the status quo as one option. For each, give costs/investments, timelines, and key tradeoffs. Recommend one and say why it beats the alternatives — including doing nothing.
5. **Next steps.** Who does what, by when.

Missing any of these → expect a bounce-back. Use the project template — it exists so the structure is automatic and a proposal takes 20–30 minutes to draft, not a week.

### Anything going to the board, investors, or external audiences

A different bar applies once a number or commitment leaves the building:

- **Conservative on timeline and cost.** If you're not safe-bet sure, don't put it in the deck. Once the board hears it, we own it — there's no graceful retraction.
- **One narrative across functions.** Tech, Product, Ops, and Marketing must reconcile their views *before* we present, not in front of the board. If I see contradiction in the room, I'll stop and align us — but I'd rather not have to.
- **Conservative externally ≠ unambitious internally.** Internal stretch goals stay internal. External commitments are the floor, not the aspiration.
- **Anticipate the specific challenger.** Map which board member will push on what, and prepare for that lens specifically — not a generic version of the answer.

## 8. Quality bar & red flags

Run this checklist before sending me work:
- [ ] **Context laid out before the solution** — diagnosis first, prescription second
- [ ] **Outcome metrics, not output metrics** ("moved X by N", not "ran Y campaigns")
- [ ] **Success criteria defined upfront** — not retrofitted after the data comes in
- [ ] At least one alternative considered, **including the status quo** ("do nothing")
- [ ] Costs and timelines per option
- [ ] Recommendation stated up front (after context)
- [ ] Numbers checked; sources linked
- [ ] The ask is explicit ("I need X by Y")

**Bounce-back triggers:**
- Going straight to the solution without diagnosis
- Output metrics dressed up as outcomes
- Success criteria invented after seeing the results (HARKing)
- Proposals with one option, or no comparison to "do nothing"
- "Just wanted to share" with no ask
- Decks where the point is on slide 8
- Numbers without source links
- Anything that should have been a paragraph but is a deck
- A result presented without the inputs that produced it (spend, headcount, one-off events)
- A board-bound commitment that isn't conservative on timeline and cost
- Function leaders presenting contradictory views in the same forum without a reconciled position

## 10. Working preferences

- **Working hours:** 9am–6pm (commercial hours). Outside that, send anything genuinely urgent — don't wait until morning.
- **Calendar:** Open and always up to date. If a slot isn't blocked, it's bookable. Treat the calendar as the source of truth.
- **Calendar blocks:** I block time for meeting prep, thinking, or specific work. You can override these if you really need to — *except* anything tagged `PERSONAL`, which is a hard block.
- **Early/late meetings:** I'll take 8am or 6–7pm slots for urgent matters. Just ask.
- **In meetings:** I concentrate fully and don't multitask. Don't expect Slack or email replies during a meeting.

### How my communication scales

Most of my direct communication goes through the leadership tier — directors and C-level — and I rely on them to cascade context to their teams. This isn't distance; it's how I trade depth for breadth. If you're outside that tier and feel out of the loop, ping your leader first. If context is genuinely missing, surface it to me directly via WhatsApp or office hours — that's a signal I want.

### Default to sharing with your team

Information from leadership-tier discussions is shareable with your team **unless the speaker explicitly says otherwise.** This is the working assumption — opt-out, not opt-in. If something is genuinely sensitive (M&A, comp, individual personnel), the person presenting will say "this stays here." Otherwise, take it back to your team. The reason is operating speed: cascading context fast beats over-protecting it.

Use judgment for items that are clearly strategic or pre-decisional. When in doubt, ask the presenter in the moment.

### Communication style: I like to be in the know

Bias toward over-communicating with me, not under. Don't filter updates because you're "not sure if it's worth my time" — it usually is.

Think of issues on a traffic-light scale, and bring them to me **early**:

- **🟢 Green — things are working, FYI:** Send it. I want context even when nothing's wrong.
- **🟡 Yellow — something brewing, risk emerging, a decision is drifting:** Bring it to me *now*, not after it turns red. Catching things at yellow is the whole point.
- **🔴 Red — blocking, customer-impacting, or time-critical:** We stop and deal.

**Emoji as acknowledgment.** I often reply with just a ✅ or 🙏. That means "got it, nothing needed from me" — it's not dismissal. Don't read more into it.

### Response & channel norms

- **Response SLO (commercial hours):** 4 hours across channels.
- **Messages are not interruptions.** Send me a message any time — Slack, email, WhatsApp — even outside hours and even when I'm in a meeting, knowing I may only see it later.
- **If you think it's worth me knowing immediately, just call me.** WhatsApp call. If I don't answer, call again. The judgment is yours; I trust it.
- **Channel guidance:**
  - **🔴 Red / time-sensitive:** WhatsApp. Call if it genuinely can't wait.
  - **🟡 Yellow / normal work:** Slack DM or email.
  - **🟢 Green / for the record:** email or doc comments.
